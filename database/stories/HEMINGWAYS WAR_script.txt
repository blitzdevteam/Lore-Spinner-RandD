Hemingway's War
By
Rand Soares
The true story of how America's favorite badass author formed his
own irregular army during WWII in order to be the first into Paris
and liberate the Ritz.

CHAPTER ONE - ITALY
EXT. AMBULANCE UNIT SECTION 4, MILAN ITALY - 1917
Ominous shadows stretch across WHITE MEDIEVAL BUILDINGS
giving off a soft summer light to the charming old CITY.
Its narrow streets jammed with ITALIAN SOLDIERS.
Rifles slung over one shoulder. Proudly marching in LINE
to what will be their inevitable deaths.
Passing a small marble structure and 18 year-old American
ERNEST HEMINGWAY, standing tall and fit.
Glaring at the SOLDIERS.
Unhappy to say the least. Jealous, ready for war. Dressed
in a clean tight neck olive drab ARMY UNIFORM.
HEMINGWAY
Look at them. Heading to the front
like real men. While I sit here on
my ass passing out cigarettes and
chocolate. Miles from any action.
Beside him, leaning against the door-frame stands fellow
American solider TED BRUMBACH (20), forced to endure
Hemingway’s endless gripe.
TED
I heard you already. We’re the
Ambulance Corp. What’d you expect?
Just be glad you’re not sent out
to get slaughtered like those poor
bastards. Most won’t be comin’
back.
HEMINGWAY
They’re soldiers-- That’s their
job.
Transfixed on their WEAPONS and EQUIPMENT.
HEMINGWAY
Illinois War Department told me I
had a vision problem. Rejected me.
I joined this damn unit so I could
get close to the action!
Enjoying the rhythmic CLAP of the SOLDIERS BOOTS marching
in unison.

TED
Why the hell would you wanna do
that?
HEMINGWAY
It’s like that book “The Dark
Forest” by Hugh Walpole. Ever read
it?
TED
Yeah, I read it. So?
HEMINGWAY
I wanna be that book.
TED
That book’s a tragedy, Ernest. You
don’t really want to be like that
book, do you? I thought you’d want
to be a Doctor, like your Father.
HEMINGWAY
Hell no! Deal with sick miserable
people for a living? No thanks.
I’m going to be a writer. That’s
what I’ve always wanted to do.
Since high school.
This news brings a hearty laugh from Ted.
TED
A writer? You? I don’t know. I
can’t quite see Ernest Hemingway
being a writer.
Both whipping their heads around when they hear WOMEN
approaching from the other side of the BUILDING.
FOUR young dark haired NURSES dressed in WHITE. And all
of them giving large infectious smiles as they pass.
HEMINGWAY
Good day ladies. You’re looking
radiantly attractive this fine
afternoon.
The ambitious Hemingway walking alongside them. Singling
one out. A petite, short haired, almost masculine WOMAN.
HEMINGWAY
You especially, Ellie. You’re like
that girl I told you about, who
looks very much like you back in
Oak Park. She was a sweet thing
named Margaret. The talk of the
town really.

Receiving a blank stare in return.
HEMINGWAY
But I’m willing to bet you’re
sweeter than ole’ Oak Park
Margaret any day aren’t you?
TED
Um-- Ernest-- You do know they
can’t understand anything you’re
saying, right? Only Italian.
HEMINGWAY
She understands me perfectly. My
speech is in a universal tone of
affection. Transcending all
nationalities. Even in this war
zone.
Extending a bent arm to Ellie, who instantly hooks it
with hers.
Happily walking down the cobblestone STREET together
toward the RED CROSS HOSPITAL a quarter mile away.
INT. AMBULANCE UNIT SECTION 4 HQ - NIGHT
What you would expect. Small, dusty, but practical.
Limited CANDLES and KEROSINE LAMPS the only source of
light.
SOLDIERS sitting under the dim glow. Including Hemingway,
Ted and five other MEN. Sorting CANDY and CIGARETTES at a
long wood table.
Each with their own PILE.
HEMINGWAY
--because you try too hard, Teddy.
Women get scared away. Especially
ones who don’t speak our language.
It’s the tone. The essence.
TED
Not that universal affection shit
again. That’s a bunch of crap and
you know it.
HEMINGWAY
Oh really. Who got a kiss today?
Quickly interrupted by heavyset Army Capt. JAMES WOODBERN
(50’s), entering from the only DOOR.
Forcing everyone to their feet. Stiff and straight.

CAPT. WOODBERN
At-ease. I need a volunteer to go
work a Canteen in the Piave
Valley. North of Venice.
This actually perks Hemingway up. Looking at Ted.
HEMINGWAY
(quietly)
That’s near the front.
Raising his hand.
HEMINGWAY
I’ll volunteer, sir.
CAPT. WOODBERN
Of course you would, Hemingway.
You’ll leave in the morning.
HEMINGWAY
Thank you, sir.
Ted pulling his arm down.
TED
You’re crazy, Ernest. What’s the
matter with you? What are your
parents going to say?
HEMINGWAY
I don’t care. This is my chance to
get near the action.
TED
Volunteer means dangerous. Don’t
stick your neck out--
And stops. The entire room instantly goes silent.
Every MAN perfectly still. Looking up.
A LOW DRONE SOUND from approaching AIRCRAFT. Getting
closer. LOUDER.
SOLDIERS quickly snuff out the LIGHTS.
Both Ted and Hemingway share a quick glance, then--
BOOM! BOOM! BOOM! The building RATTLES and SHAKES from an
AERIAL BOMBING attack further away.
Everyone diving under the tables.
Capt. Woodbern rushes to one of the WINDOWS. Hemingway
and Ted to another.

TED
(stunned)
The hospital!
HEMINGWAY
Oh, no--
Rushing out the DOOR.
EXT. RED CROSS HOSPITAL - NIGHT
Totally destroyed. All walls collapsed. FIRES everywhere
creating an eerie light exposing BODY PARTS littering the
grounds.
Bloody PIECES hanging from a snarled BARBED WIRE FENCE
encircling the property.
More PIECES mixed into smoking piles of RUBBLE.
The MEN of Ambulance Section Unit 4 stand frozen in
horror at the grizzly sight before them.
Each holding clean, unused STRETCHERS.
CAPT. WOODBERN
Alright, get to it. Retrieve every
piece. No matter how small. Bring
them back here to the trucks-- Go
on!
His reluctant MEN slowly regain their nerve. Still trying
to comprehend the horrific task.
And get to work. Gathering BODY PARTS.
Placing them onto STRETCHERS PIECE by PIECE. Until each
one is full. Walking them back to the TRUCKS.
Including Hemingway. Finding LARGE BITS of PATIENTS and
NURSES.
HEMINGWAY
I’ve never seen a woman in this
condition before have you, Teddy?
Who’s ready to vomit. Unable to look down.
HEMINGWAY
It’s something I’ve never really
thought of before. And frankly,
find a little disturbing.

Discovering the severed HEAD and TORSO of ELLIE-- Which
really disturbs him. Almost to the core. Causing him to
pause.
Sadly reflecting on her short, beautiful life and his own
mortality.
Then carefully picks her up. Carrying the PIECES to a
STRETCHER.
Other MEN in his Unit have had enough. Falling to their
knees, VOMITING. Unable to continue.
EXT. PIAVE RIVER VALLEY CANTEEN - DAY
Small makeshift WOOD and CANVAS STRUCTURE for soldiers to
relax, have coffee, cigarettes and candy.
Run by American and Italian VOLUNTEERS all listening to
sounds of large EXPLOSIONS in the distance.
Commanding the Aid Station is no-nonsense Capt. JAMES
GAMBLE (40’s). A slender man with cheerful demeanor,
which serves him well in a hellish post like this.
And Capt. Gamble loves his new, eager volunteer.
CAPT. GAMBLE
Hemingway!
Who rushes to his side.
HEMINGWAY
Yes, sir?
CAPT. GAMBLE
I want you to ride down to a
forward listening post along the
west bank. Pass out chocolate.
Think you can handle it?
HEMINGWAY
(enthusiastically)
Yes, sir!
CAPT. GAMBLE
Okay-- Get moving. It’ll take you
a while to get there. You don’t
want to be riding around in the
dark so close to the front.
HEMINGWAY
On my way.

Grabbing one of several BICYCLES leaning against a tree,
happily filling its WIRE BASKET with chocolate.
Then mounts up. Quickly RIDING down a narrow dirt road
toward the surrounding MOUNTAIN RANGE.
EXT. LISTENING POST, PIAVE RIVER - NIGHT
A ghostly apocalyptic sight. Mud filled BOMB CRATERS dot
the once beautiful landscape. DUG-OUTS, deep PITS for
soldiers to hide in, make up the remaining ground.
Only forty-yards from the Austrian Lines. EXPLODING
SHELLS getting dangerously close.
Two dozen dirty and fatigued Italian SOLDIERS stand
ready. Waiting to give their lives.
Some above ground. Others inside the PITS.
Up top, making his way to each DUG OUT comes Hemingway.
Handing out chocolate to the terrified, but grateful MEN.
DUG OUT
More of a muddy nightmare. Two SOLDIERS balance
themselves along the edge. Rifles pointing toward the
Enemy.
Both tense. Alert. Scared.
Down slides Hemingway. With warm smile and handful of
chocolate.
Giving the poor MEN a quick reprieve. If only for a
moment.
HEMINGWAY
Hey there! How you boys doin’?
Been down here a long time? I hope
this chocolate--
Stopped by the WHISTLING SOUND of a MORTAR SHELL coming
in fast.
BOOM! EXPLODING between Hemingway and the two MEN with a
DEAFENING SHRAPNEL LEYDEN LIGHT.
Instantly CUTTING one SOLDIER INTO THREE PIECES.
The other has BOTH LEGS BLOWN OFF, sending him across the
PIT, SCREAMING.
Hemingway’s thrown into the side. SHRAPNEL in his LEGS,
HAND and HEAD.

Knocking him unconscious.
The horrifying SCREAMS from the LEGLESS MAN slowly get
weaker. Quieter. Then fall silent as he BLEEDS OUT.
Two more SOLDIERS jump in and grab Hemingway.
Carrying him away.
Until a second SHELL comes WHISTLING overhead. Making
both terrified MEN drop him and take cover as it passes.
Then pick him up-- only to drop him again when a third
SHELL flies over.
Finally, carrying the now fully conscious Hemingway to an
AID STATION.
That looks more like a BUTCHER SHOP than Medical Unit.
Setting him down next to a dozen badly wounded ITALIAN
SOLDIERS.
In much worse shape than he is.
Waving off the DOCTOR who tries to care for him.
HEMINGWAY
Not me, Doc. Treat them first. I
can wait. Those guys need it more
than me.
Pointing to agonizing SOLDIERS. Some MISSING LIMBS.
Others bleeding profusely from multiple BULLET WOUNDS.
An abundance of pain and misery to go around.
INT. AMERICAN HOSPITAL, MILAN - LATE DAY
Everything WHITE, clean and sterile. In stark contrast to
the bloody battlefield Aid Station the day before.
Quiet and pleasant. Sun shinning through all twelve
windows giving a calming glow to everything it touches.
Including one full wall of BEDS. Filled with WOUNDED
SOLDIERS.
Hemingway one of them.
His HEAD, LEGS and HAND BANDAGED with gauze. Not too bad,
considering the company he’s in.
Sitting beside him is Ted. The only non patient. And the
only one not wearing white.

Holding a NOTEPAD and PEN. Writing a letter.
TED
“And after crawling on my arms to
help a soldier who just lost both
legs, I carried the two gravely
wounded men out from the pit of
death on my back. Even though I
myself was badly wounded.”
Flipping to a clean page, continuing to write. Hemingway
loving every word, despite the outright lie.
TED
“Then I refused medical treatment
so others could be saved, earning
the Italian Medal of Valor for my
actions.”
HEMINGWAY
I did all that, huh?
TED
Sure. Why not? Your father will
love it.
HEMINGWAY
The Army’s sending me home.
TED
I heard. Sure wish I was going.
HEMINGWAY
But I don’t wanna go. At least not
yet. And certainly not back to my
old job at the Kansas City Star.
TED
What’s wrong with that? You said
you wanted to be a writer.
HEMINGWAY
Yeah, sure, but not for a Paper. I
need something more exciting. More
action!
TED
After all this shit? You’re still
talkin’ action? I think you’ve
cracked, Ernest. Maybe you really
are that book.
A disappointed Hemingway snatches the embellished LETTER.
SIGNING at the bottom.
Tossing it back.

Looking up through a LARGE WINDOW toward the BRIGHT
SETTING SUN.
DISSOLVE TO:
CHAPTER TWO - PARIS
EXT. THE RITZ - 1937
A BRIGHT SETTING SUN envelopes long immaculate GARDENS,
SCULPTURES, FLOWERS and perfectly trimmed HEDGES.
All ending at the base of the beautiful PLACE VENDOME
that Louis XIV eventually finished in 1705.
Large BRICK LINED COURTYARD leading to the HOTEL portion
of this massive three sided PALACE-- standing THREE
MAGNIFICENT STORIES with wide ARCHED WINDOWS, AWNINGS,
crafted MOLDING and LOW IRON RAILINGS.
Three carpeted steps to the tall ATRIUM and Hotel’s WOOD
REVOLVING DOOR.
INT. THE RITZ LOBBY - SUNSET
VAULTED CEILINGS. MARBLE FLOORS. Massive PERSIAN RUGS.
HARDWOOD and BRASS FIXTURES throughout.
Every GUEST dressed in the latest fashion. Almost
becoming part of the decor.
A long, sweeping STAIRCASE near the front, accented with
GILDED SPIRAL RAILING.
Victorian PORTRAITS, TAPESTRIES and VELVET FURNITURE
round out the remainder of this majestic Hotel.
Tucked away on the other side--
The two BARS.
One large, the other more intimate.
SMALL BAR
Known as the LADIES BAR. With LOW CEILING, CROWN MOLDING
and multiple FANS spinning in unison.
Circulating the smoke filled air.
Also sporting a two-step UPPER LEVEL toward the back.

With long, curved, TAN LEATHER PADDED BAR on one side,
stocked with everything.
Complete with PADDED STOOLS and HIGH-BACK CHAIRS giving
an intellectual appearance.
Perfect for writers.
At one of the cozy tables, now wearing glasses sits 37
year-old ERNEST HEMINGWAY looking a bit fuller in the
face and body since the last time we saw him.
Drinking a DRY MARTINI next to F. SCOTT FITZGERALD (39),
GERTRUDE STEIN (57) and ALICE B. TOKLAS (59), who all
drink red wine.
And each a literary heavyweight in their own right.
HEMINGWAY
I’m just saying Wescott’s “The
Apple of the Eye” was deeply
flawed.
STEIN
How so?
HEMINGWAY
His pros style’s fake. And stories
fundamentally unsound. Making his
affected speaking-- nauseating.
TOKLAS
You’re still angry he made you
change that character in “The Sun
Also Rises.”
HEMINGWAY
It wasn’t anything like his
character! He’s just jealous my
book made a fortune and his
didn’t.
FITZGERALD
You do have a habit of using your
friends for your stories.
HEMINGWAY
Not you too, Francis.
STEIN
He’s right, Ernest. You used
entire conversations of ours.
HEMINGWAY
That’s nonsense. I did no such
thing.

STEIN
The hell you didn’t! I read big
pieces of my life in your books.
TOKLAS
As did I.
Making Hemingway glare at Fitzgerald. Who remains silent.
HEMINGWAY
Alright. Maybe a little. But it
was done in good taste. To benefit
the story and main character.
FITZGERALD
I think we would like it best if
you would at least change the
names next time. Be less obvious
about it.
HEMINGWAY
I won’t promise anything. Besides,
you’re the one who thought you had
a better ending than mine. What a
bunch of shit!
As he takes the last gulp of his drink.
Raising his hand. SIGNALING the middle-aged BARTENDER
dressed like one of the flying monkey’s from The Wizard
of Oz.
STEIN
I remember when you could only
afford a single drink here,
Ernest.
TOKLAS
You and Hadley didn’t have much of
anything back then. Even that
apartment on the West Bank was
pretty dreary. But you were a
lovely couple.
HEMINGWAY
The twenties were lean years. No
doubt. But, if you remember, the
American dollar was pretty high
against the Franc. So it wasn’t
that bad, I suppose.
FITZGERALD
Paris was a lot of fun back then.
I miss it. Sad how things have
changed.

HEMINGWAY
Yeah, two ex wives and three
children.
Producing a slight chuckle from the Ladies.
STEIN
How are the boys, Ernest?
HEMINGWAY
They’re doing fine. I’ve been a
bit distant, though. Kind’a feel
bad about it, too. I’m not much
for children, but these boys are
really good kids! I saw them
before I left for Spain. They’re
in Wyoming with Pauline.
TOKLAS
You really think the war in Spain
will spill over and become larger?
HEMINGWAY
Most definitely. The German’s and
Italian’s are just testing their
armies. They have a bigger plan in
mind.
STEIN
Is that what you’re reporting to
the States?
HEMINGWAY
Not anymore. The Paper wants
action. Not opinion. So that’s
what I give them. Only what I see
or experience.
FITZGERALD
I’m curious-- How can you go back
to war after what you’ve been
through?
TOKLAS
He’s right. After seeing all that
death and bloodshed?
HEMINGWAY
In the big war, there were things
that scared me. Now there’s not.
I’m a man who needs to get close
to the action! Even if it means
working for a Paper again to get
there.

STEIN
You’re a tortured soul, Ernest.
Your work proves that. You
should’ve been a soldier.
HEMINGWAY
Not according to the War
Department.
Tipping his GLASSES.
FITZGERALD
When do you go back to Spain?
HEMINGWAY
In two days. That’s where things
are really different. All my old
contacts have been telling me
what’s going on.
Finally receiving his fresh MARTINI.
HEMINGWAY
Gone are the spectacular
bullfights and trout fishing.
TOKLAS
Bullfighting is so barbaric,
Ernest. How can you watch that?
This actually offends him.
HEMINGWAY
Nonsense! Bullfighting is a manly
sport. One against the beast.
Overcoming death.
TOKLAS
It’s cruel and disgusting.
HEMINGWAY
(ignoring her)
Like my safaris in Africa. One on
one against large animals. The
hunt-- the kill.
Gulping down half his DRINK.
TOKLAS
That’s even worse. Hunting those
poor creatures.
STEIN
How can you do such horrible
things, yet write such beautiful
words?

HEMINGWAY
I triumph over fear. Spain’s
Manuel Manolo Garcia is the best
bullfighter in the world. He’s a
real man! Fearless!
(sadly)
But that damn civil war ruined
everything.
FITZGERALD
You sound like the perfect choice
to cover it.
HEMINGWAY
War is the best outdoor sport
there is.
STEIN
That’s absurd.
HEMINGWAY
Any man not concerned with
violence or death is a sissy!
In walks a young, nicely dressed IRWIN SHAW (23), who
hesitates before joining them.
FITZGERALD
Irwin, take a chair.
Helping him slide another LEATHER PADDED SEAT next to the
table.
FITZGERALD
You’ve read some of Irwin’s short
stories, haven’t you Ernest?
HEMINGWAY
I have. Liked them. You’ll do fine
kid.
SHAW
Thank you.
STEIN
We were discussing the civil war
in Spain.
TOKLAS
What do you think of war, Mr.
Shaw? Ernest says it’s a sport.
Putting Shaw on the spot.

SHAW
Well-- if football players were
armed with guns, there wouldn’t be
stadiums large enough to hold the
crowds.
HEMINGWAY
Here, here!
Giving him a quick toast in solidarity.
INT. LOBBY - NIGHT
Another quiet evening at the Ritz. The well dressed
Manager, CLAUDE AUZELLO (40’s) stands proudly by the
SPIRAL STAIRS.
Hemingway next to him. Both admiring the place.
HEMINGWAY
I have to tell you Claude--
Whenever I think of the afterlife
in Heaven, the action always takes
place at the Ritz.
AUZELLO
You’ve certainly spent enough time
in your life here to imagine so.
And you can finally afford our
best rooms if you wish.
HEMINGWAY
No, thanks. My old room is the
best room.
AUZELLO
Of course.
HEMINGWAY
Is she here?
AUZELLO
Checked in half hour ago.
HEMINGWAY
Thanks.
Slipping a few FRANCS into Auzello’s jacket pocket before
turning and going up.
Running into Fitzgerald on his way down.
FITZGERALD
Pauline’s in Wyoming with the
boys?

HEMINGWAY
Yeah.
FITZGERALD
Did I tell you about the time I
was courting Zelda? During the
war? She fell in love with a
French Navy pilot, Edouard Jozan.
Flew one of those seaplanes.
Following Hemingway up.
FITZGERALD
I was so in love with Zelda that
it nearly destroyed me when I
found out. Feelings I don’t ever
want to have again. No one should.
Then stops. Grabbing his arm.
FITZGERALD
I had her-- then lost her-- then
won her back again.
HEMINGWAY
Is there a point to this, Francis?
I’m busy.
FITZGERALD
Don’t neglect the one’s who care
about you. Think of their
feelings, too.
HEMINGWAY
Feelings? Like Zelda? She hates
me.
FITZGERALD
She doesn’t hate you.
HEMINGWAY
Oh yes she does! Your wife’s
crazy, Francis. She’s a cock-
teaser, but you’re always too
drunk to notice. I feel sorry for
you.
Continuing up to the SECOND FLOOR.
INT. RITZ BEDROOM - NIGHT
The soft flickering GLOW of a FIREPLACE. Like something
out of Queen Victoria’s Palace.

Vaulted ceilings. ORNATE MOLDINGS. A CURVED WALL with
tall DRAPED WINDOW.
Complete with a regal CANOPY BED.
Restlessly sleeping inside lays Hemingway. Twitching and
jerking. Having a nightmare, then--
Bolts up, SCREAMING. Sweat rolling down his face.
Breathing labored.
The WOMAN next to him, MARTHA GELLHORN (20’s), short
cropped hair with an almost masculine look, also sits up.
MARTHA
Breathe Ernest, breathe!
Hemingway’s slowly able to calm himself.
MARTHA
This is happening more often.
Maybe you should get checked out.
HEMINGWAY
I’m fine.
But his face says otherwise. Scared and vulnerable.
Going into the BATHROOM.
EXT. RITZ GARDEN - MORNING
Usual grey Autumn overcast. Birds and insects flutter
through the lush manicured plants without a care in the
world.
Unlike Hemingway, trying to look like someone without a
care in the world. Dressed in a dark tailored suit.
Stopping to smell the roses, when--
PICASSO (O.C.)
Ernest!
Turning to see his old friend PABLO PICASSO (30), with a
big smile and paint stained hand.
HEMINGWAY
Pablo!
Giving it a firm shake.
HEMINGWAY
How the hell are yea?

PICASSO
I heard you were back at the Ritz.
HEMINGWAY
Can’t keep me away too long. I’ve
been in Havana, sailing my boat.
Doing some great marlin fishing.
Both moving off through the GARDEN.
PICASSO
How’s Pauline?
HEMINGWAY
(embarrassed)
Pauline? Well--
PICASSO
Not again?
HEMINGWAY
Two ex-wives isn’t that bad.
Shooting him a boyish GRIN.
PICASSO
I wanted to talk to you about
Spain. My friends there tell me
it’s even worse than before. The
fascists are taking over.
HEMINGWAY
They’re telling you the truth.
It’s getting bad, Pablo. I fear
for the people. And all my old
contacts. I’m headed back there
tomorrow.
PICASSO
Come by my studio for lunch.
HEMINGWAY
Sure. That would be swell.
Both continuing across the floral MAZE. Out onto the
BRICK LINED COURTYARD--
Toward the HOTEL ENTRANCE.
INT. LADIES BAR - EVENING
Always a casual atmosphere. Producing a Bohemian look.
More Hemingway’s style.

Wearing a smoking jacket. Sitting at the very back,
throwing down his fourth DRY MARTINI. Martha beside him.
But wearing current fashion with a sense of non
tradition.
Across from them, looking more artistic sipping cognac
are famous Library owner SYLVIA BEACH (49) and author
T. S. ELIOT (47).
HEMINGWAY
Editors can be a pain in the ass,
Thomas. You gotta stand up to
them.
ELIOT
Easy for you to say.
HEMINGWAY
The editor of “Farewell to Arms”
told me to eliminate the words,
fuck, son of a bitch, whore, cock-
sucker, shit and Jesus Christ.
BEACH
Perkins was more of a censor than
an editor. But I did see his
point.
ELIOT
(to Hemingway)
What did you say to him?
HEMINGWAY
Lots! We went back and forth for a
while and eventually I got whore
and Jesus Christ reinstated.
ELIOT
I’m not in a position to be that
bold. Not yet anyway.
HEMINGWAY
Don’t worry. I’m sure he’ll
reprint your poems in a full
collection. That’s what they did
with my short stories. They sell
better that way. As a compilation.
More like a book.
BEACH
Your stories did a marvellous job,
Ernest. They even reprinted some.
HEMINGWAY
Twice!

BEACH
I have three copies at my library.
HEMINGWAY
How is Shakespeare and Company? I
meant to stop by before I left.
BEACH
We’re still doing what we do best.
Giving the world access to fine
literature. Including your works,
darling.
ELIOT
You’ve made quite a lot of money
with your writing. I hope to do
the same some day. Any more novels
coming?
HEMINGWAY
I’ve been working on one-- two
really, that I think will
eventually become a single book.
Seeing Gertrude Stein and F. Scott Fitzgerald enter the
BAR.
Making their way over. Fitzgerald glaring at Martha.
HEMINGWAY
And here they come.
STEIN
Good evening, Sylvia, Ernest--

A German SNIPER. Camouflaged and hidden by BRICKS from
its FIVE CHIMNEYS.
The BARREL of his RIFLE resting on a RUSTED GUTTER.
Two more GERMANS across the street watching from a FOURTH
FLOOR WINDOW.
Anti-Tank ROCKETS at their ready.
Unable to see Marcel and his FIGHTERS sneaking around the
BUILDING.
Gaining a clear vantage point.
MARCEL
(quietly)
There they are. In that upper
window.
Pointing them out.
Cuing his MEN who immediately OPEN FIRE with their
MACHINE GUNS. Peppering the entire face of the BUILDING.
The dug in German’s RETURN FIRE. Creating a small BATTLE.
Through all the chaos is Hemingway, moving along the
bottom edge of the APARTMENT BUILDING.
Directly below the GERMANS. Unnoticed.
Stopping only to swap out his empty CLIP.

Then hears SHOUTING. In English. His head on a swivel,
trying to figure out where it’s coming from.
Finally seeing an AMERICAN SOLDIER down the road,
frantically WAVING his ARMS.
Getting Hemingway’s attention.
HEMINGWAY
(yelling back)
What the hell you doing? Can’t you
see there’s a war on?
SOLDIER
Get outta there!! French tanks are
gonna blow that German position!!
HEMINGWAY
French tanks?
Looking beyond the SOLDIER-- at a FRENCH TANK heading
right for them.
Its LONG BARREL slowly raising up. Getting a clear shot
of the APARTMENTS.
HEMINGWAY
Oh shit. Red!
BOOM! The TANK FIRES--
HITTING the top of the BUILDING. Instantly killing the
SNIPER and destroying a large portion of the ROOF.
That rains down onto Hemingway, Red and Louis trying to
retreat. Knocking them to the ground.
Covering them with DEBRIS. BOOM! The TANK FIRES another
SHELL--
STRIKING the FOURTH FLOOR of the second BUILDING.
COMPLETELY DESTROYING IT.
Sending more BRICKS and deadly DEBRIS down onto the
STREET below.
The air filling with DUST. Almost impossible to see.
Running into the cloud are Marcel, George and Jacques who
pull CHUNKS of RUBBLE off the fallen MEN.
Helping them up.
MARCEL
Hurry!

The TANK almost on top of them.
Red jumps into his shot-up JEEP and quickly starts the
engine.
RED
Let’s go!
A limping Hemingway slides in the front seat. Marcel into
a TRUCK behind them.
His remaining FIGHTERS scramble into the BACK. Everyone
keeping low.
Both VEHICLES speeding off--
Around a corner. Away from more ADVANCING TANKS.
EXT. CHAMPS-ELYSEES - DAY
Swarms of PEOPLE in the STREETS holding SMALL FRENCH
FLAGS. With more coming out by the minute.
News of the German retreat spreading fast.
Cruising down the wide open boulevard comes Hemingway’s
IRREGULARS.
WAVING to those around them. WOMEN throwing FLOWERS into
their VEHICLES as they pass.
Others handing them BOTTLES of WINE or CHAMPAGNE.
HEMINGWAY
(to Red)
Slow down. It’s over here on the
right.
A stunning 19th Century ROCOCO MANSION. Parking out
front.
RED
What’s this?
HEMINGWAY
The Traveler’s Club.
RED
The what?
Marcel’s TRUCK pulls alongside them. Watching a partially
dusty Hemingway climb out of the JEEP.
Looking the place over. Patting himself clean.

MARCEL
What are we doing here? The Ritz
is over there!
HEMINGWAY
I’ll only be minute.
Heading for the FRONT DOORS.
INT. TRAVELERS CLUB - CONTINUOUS
Like the Ritz. An exquisite Palace. With huge crystal
CHANDELIER hanging over the stunning LOBBY.
Massive PAINTINGS line the GOLD TINTED WALLS.
Tranquil and quiet.
Until Hemingway walks through the IRON DOORS. As if he
owns the place, proclaiming--
HEMINGWAY
Where the hell can a man get a
drink around here?
And heads across the beautiful PERSIAN RUG, past original
VICTORIAN CHAIRS.
Through a tall hand crafted WOOD DOOR.
DINNING ROOM/BAR
Just as stunning as the Lobby. Hand carved hardwood and
burgundy VELVET COUCHES surrounding antique library
CHAIRS.
Occupied by Alice B. Tolkas, Gertrude Stein, Sylvia Beach
and MYRTLE WEBB (20’s).
All having DRINKS. Toasting an end to National Socialist
rule.
Rough and ready Army Col. TOBY MILLER (40’s) by the BAR.
Everyone suddenly turning. As the DOOR bursts OPEN. In
struts Hemingway. Spotting Col. Miller.
HEMINGWAY
For God sake, Toby! Get me a
whiskey!
Marching over to him.

COL. MILLER
Ernest! David Bruce said you were
heading this way.
Quickly signaling the young 30 something BARTENDER for
the drink.
Who pours it and hands the GLASS to smiling Hemingway.
HEMINGWAY
Much appreciated.
Taking a well deserved GULP.
HEMINGWAY
How the hell did you get here so
fast?
COL. MILLER
Patton sent me ahead with a small
scout team. Verify Paris was open.
STEIN
Welcome back, Ernest. Nice to see
you’re still in one piece.
HEMINGWAY
It’ll take more than a bunch of
fanatical Germans to bring me
down, Gertrude.
Joining them at the COUCHES.
Eyeballing Myrtle. Who stares back at him with star
struck admiration.
HEMINGWAY
And who might you be?
BEACH
My assistant, Myrtle Webb.
Holding her arm out as an introduction.
BEACH
Myrtle, this is Ernest Hemingway.
MYRTLE
It’s an honor, Mr. Hemingway.
Pleasantly SHAKING his much larger hand.
TOKLAS
Calm yourself, dear. He’s a wolf
in sheep’s clothing.

Myrtle doesn’t care. Hemingway’s a legend to her.
TOKLAS
Still covering this dreadful war,
Ernest?
HEMINGWAY
Of course.
STEIN
Is it true you stole that Colliers
assignment from your Wife, Mary?
HEMINGWAY
Come on Gertrude, I don’t need to
steal a war assignment like this.
I’m the best there is.
Giving himself a TOAST. Taking another drink.
STEIN
Certainly can’t argue with that.
TOKLAS
What brings you here?
HEMINGWAY
I’m on my way to liberate the
Ritz. And kill every damn German
there!
TOKLAS
I see.
STEIN
You’ve finally become a soldier
have you. You did always want to
be one-- more or less.
HEMINGWAY
No, I’m still just a war
corespondent.
COL. MILLER
He’s not allowed to be a soldier,
Miss Stein. It’s against the
rules. Patton had him arrested and
confined to quarters because of
it.
Which gives Hemingway a quick pause.
HEMINGWAY
George was just doing what he was
told.

BEACH
You don’t look very confined to
quarters.
Col. Miller raises an eyebrow. Also wondering why he’s
not.
HEMINGWAY
That’s Top Secret information.
STEIN
How are you planning on liberating
the Ritz when the rules forbid you
from doing so?
HEMINGWAY
I have a small army of French
partisan fighters waiting outside
to help facilitate the matter.
COL. MILLER
That would do it.
Holding up his GLASS.
CRASH! The tall DOOR swings open. Red rushes in. Out of
breath.
RED
Leclerc’s coming up the road!
Patton’s whole 3rd Army is right
behind him!
HEMINGWAY
Shit! Sorry, ladies. I gotta run.
Pounding the remainder of his Whisky. Slamming the GLASS
on the BAR.
MYRTLE
Can I come with you?
BEACH
Myrtle! Are you crazy?
MYRTLE
Liberating the Ritz sounds like
fun.
BEACH
It’s dangerous! There’s still
Germans out there.
MYRTLE
I can take care of myself, Sylvia.
Thank you for your concern.

Immediately jumping up from the COUCH.
MYRTLE
(to Hemingway)
Let’s go.
HEMINGWAY
Have a good war, Toby!
COL. MILLER
You too, Ernest.
Watching him and Myrtle exit the ROOM.
EXT. CHAMPS-ELYSEES - DAY
The FIGHTERS impatiently wait in the TRUCK. Drinking
straight from the BOTTLES.
Sounds of HEAVY TANKS approaching.
Hemingway, Myrtle and Red finally appear jumping into the
JEEP. Marcel already behind the wheel of the TRUCK.
MARCEL
They’re coming!
HEMINGWAY
I know!
Red steps on the GAS. Speeding away from the Travelers
Club.
And approaching ARMY.
Myrtle holding on for dear life as the JEEP slides around
a CORNER onto a SMALLER STREET.
Slamming on the BRAKES--
Almost running into a LINE OF FRENCH TANKS.
HEMINGWAY
Back up! Back up!
Frantically waving his hand to Marcel, who jams it into
REVERSE and drives backwards.
Onto the MAIN ROAD. The JEEP right behind him.
Both VEHICLES sliding 180 degrees to an abrupt stop.
Facing forward.
Then step on it. Trying to put distance between them and
the Allied ARMY.

Immediately coming under enemy SNIPER FIRE. Forcing them

Quickly turning down an ALLEY. Barely able to fit. The

Exiting out onto another NARROW STREET.
That Hemingway recognizes immediately.
HEMINGWAY
I know where we are. Pull over
here!
Pointing to a spot outside a YELLOW TWO STORY SQUARE

Multiple WINDOWS encircle the flat TOP. A curious

MYRTLE
Where you going? This isn’t the

Following him to the DOOR.
HEMINGWAY
I wanna check on someone.
Then KNOCKS. Waiting--
The DOOR slowly OPENS a couple inches. Half a FACE

HEMINGWAY
You alive Pablo?
As the DOOR swings fully OPEN. Pablo Picasso standing on

PICASSO
Ernest! My friend!
Throwing both ARMS around the big man.
PICASSO
Come in! Come in!
Leading them inside.
PICASSO’S STUDIO
Plenty of LIGHT and COLOR. Various artistic EXPERIMENTS

All banned by the Nazis and painted in secret.
The most current WORKS leaning against the WALLS or up on

PICASSO
What are you doing back in Paris?
HEMINGWAY
I’m here to liberate my Ritz! And

Which stops Picasso.
PICASSO
Of course you are.
CLAPPING his hands. Bursting into LAUGHTER.
Across the ROOM, a young 23 year-old female “Art Student”

FRANCOISE
What’s so funny about that. I love

PICASSO
This is my student, Francoise

Looking at Myrtle.
MYRTLE
Myrtle Webb. Sylvia Beach’s

Slowly extending her small delicate hand.
PICASSO
Ah yes. I heard she had a new hire

Clutching it tight.
HEMINGWAY
Speaking of new-- Where’s that

I grew tired of her a couple years

Looking Myrtle up and down. Still holding her hand.

What?

MYRTLE
Wait a minute.
PICASSO
Francoise!
She dashes from the DOORWAY. Switching out a fresh WHITE

I have to go liberate the Ritz,

PICASSO

PICASSO

MYRTLE

He’s an artist. They say things

Beautiful as always. Untouched by the war. GARDENS

Everything looks as it did in the 30’s.

Unaware of Hemingway and his FIGHTERS a few hundred yards

Watching them with BINOCULARS.
HEMINGWAY
I count five.
MARCEL
With more inside I’m sure.
HEMINGWAY

HEMINGWAY

Claude Auzello, the Ritz Manager, stepping out from the

MARCEL
Leave our weapons at the door?

Charging up the STEPS.
INT. THE RITZ - CONTINUOUS

(pointing)

Racing up the STAIRS.

THIRD FLOOR

HEMINGWAY
(in French)
Out! Get out! All German’s get the

Kicking OPEN any DOOR he finds locked.

MARCEL
All clear downstairs.
HEMINGWAY
Now to my favorite bar.
Heading across the Hotel. Red, Marcel and Louis right

LADIES BAR

This place is hereby officially

HEMINGWAY
Give me fifty Martinis! On the

HEMINGWAY

Mission accomplished.
HEMINGWAY
To our lost brothers. And a job

Lightly touching DRINKS. Pouring them down their throats.

Happily putting his arms around Red and Marcel--
FREEZE FRAME
On Hemingway. His colorful face beaming.
Enjoying the triumphant liberation of his beloved Ritz

Everything gradually TURNING TO BLACK AND WHITE.
DISSOLVE TO:
INT. THE RITZ, LADIES BAR - PRESENT DAY
The EXACT SAME FRAME only it’s a BLACK AND WHITE PHOTO

“BAR HEMINGWAY.”
Next to other PHOTOS of him during WWI, FISHING,
BULLFIGHTING, BOXING, SPANISH CIVIL WAR and WWII.

Including two sets of SHARK JAWS out of Key West.

The space still furnished with fine TAN LEATHER BENCH

Tastefully displayed behind the LEATHER PADDED BAR, more

Smiling at all who enter his favorite place, worthy of
